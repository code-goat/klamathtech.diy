# Your Browser Is Watching You

Most people use Google Chrome. And Chrome is excellent at one thing in particular: reporting everything you do back to Google.

Every site you visit, every search you type, every link you click — Chrome logs it and sends it to one of the largest advertising corporations on earth. This is not a bug. It is the product. You are not using Chrome for free. You are paying with your data.

The good news: switching to a more private browser takes about five minutes.

---

## The Problem with Chrome (and Some Others)

**Google Chrome** is built and maintained by Google, whose entire business model depends on knowing as much as possible about you to serve targeted ads. Chrome is fast and widely compatible, but it is fundamentally a data collection tool.

**Microsoft Edge** is built on the same underlying technology as Chrome and has similar privacy issues, including built-in telemetry that sends your browsing data to Microsoft.

**Safari** (Apple's browser) is meaningfully better than Chrome for privacy — Apple is less dependent on advertising revenue — but it is only available on Apple devices and still has some tracking.

---

## What We Recommend

### Firefox — Best All-Around Choice

**Firefox** is free, open-source, and maintained by the Mozilla Foundation — a nonprofit, not an advertising company. It does not profile you or sell your data.

Out of the box, Firefox is decent. With a few settings changes and one extension, it becomes very good.

**Get Firefox:** [firefox.com](https://www.mozilla.org/firefox/)

**After installing, do this:**

1. Install the **uBlock Origin** extension — it blocks ads and trackers. It is free, open-source, and one of the most effective privacy tools available. Find it in the Firefox Add-ons store by searching "uBlock Origin."
2. Go to **Settings → Privacy & Security** and set Enhanced Tracking Protection to **Strict**.
3. Set your default search engine to something other than Google (see the [next guide](/guides/privacy101/3-search/) for options).
4. Optional: enable **DNS over HTTPS** in Settings → Privacy & Security → scroll to bottom. This prevents your internet provider from seeing which sites you look up.

### Brave — Solid Alternative

**Brave** is a browser built on the same base as Chrome (so it's compatible with Chrome extensions), but with strong privacy protections built in by default — ad blocking, tracker blocking, and fingerprinting protection out of the box.

Brave does have some marketing that can feel gimmicky (they have their own cryptocurrency rewards program you can safely ignore), but the core browser is genuinely privacy-respecting.

**Get Brave:** [brave.com](https://brave.com/)

---

## On Mobile

**On Android:** Firefox for Android with uBlock Origin, or Brave.

**On iPhone/iPad:** Firefox Focus for simple, automatic private browsing. Brave also works well. Safari with iCloud Private Relay (if you have an iCloud+ subscription) is a reasonable option.

Avoid using Chrome on mobile for the same reasons as desktop.

---

## One More Thing: Private Browsing Mode

"Private" or "Incognito" mode does **not** make you anonymous online. It only prevents your browser from saving your history *locally* on your device. Your internet provider, the sites you visit, and Google (if you're using Chrome) can still see everything you do. Private mode is useful for borrowing someone's device or shopping for gifts — it is not a privacy tool.

Real privacy comes from the browser itself and the extensions you run, not from a special mode.
